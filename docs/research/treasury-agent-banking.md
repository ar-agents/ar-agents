# Treasury and banking for an agent-operated society

Research synthesis for `ROADMAP.md` item M2-4 (Society treasury and banking). This document answers one question: how should a Sociedad Automatizada, an AI-agent-operated business under Argentina's draft `art. 14` regime, hold and move money today, before the law is live and before the operator is an incorporated company. It is written for a bootstrapped, non-lawyer founder deciding what to build this quarter, and it is meant to be read alongside a lawyer before any real money moves at scale.

**How to read the citations.** Every external claim links to its source. Claims that went through this research pass's adversarial verification step are marked `[verified 3-0]` or `[verified 2-0]` when the corpus recorded an explicit refute-or-survive vote; most of those are about the wallet-provider landscape (Coinbase, Circle, Privy, Turnkey). Claims marked `[single source]` come from one fetched source with no independent corroboration in this corpus. Claims marked `[UNVERIFIED, not adversarially checked]` are primary-source (Boletín Oficial, vendor docs) but this research pass did not run a verification round on them, chiefly the Argentine central-bank and AML material in Section 3, so treat their reading of the regulation as a starting point for legal review, not a legal conclusion.

## 1. Question and constraints

The founder (Nazareno Clemente) is a monotributista (Cat. A, servicios informáticos) with no incorporated company. `ar-agents` already has, in the repo, working pieces of the answer:

- **x402 USDC intake proven on Base Sepolia.** `@ar-agents/x402` implements the x402 v1 spec (payment requirements, `X-PAYMENT` headers, facilitator verify/settle) as a buyer and seller helper, Edge-Runtime compatible, with wallets deliberately kept outside the package (`packages/x402/README.md`).
- **A treasury package** (`packages/treasury/src/index.ts`) that tracks USDC/ARS balances, sizes an ARS tax buffer against AFIP obligations, plans just-in-time USDC to ARS conversion, and accounts for the cedular tax on disposals. It defines an `OffRampAdapter` interface (`quote`, `convert`, `getStatus`) with four concrete implementations already wired: `BitsoOffRampAdapter`, `MantecaOffRampAdapter`, `RipioOffRampAdapter`, and `MuralOffRampAdapter`, plus an idempotency wrapper (`withOffRampIdempotency`) so a retried conversion never double-pays. The package's own comment is explicit about the legal posture: "we integrate one [PSAV], we do not become one (CNV RG 1058/2025)."
- **A Mercado Pago package** for ARS collection and (per `ROADMAP.md` M2-1) subscription billing.
- **An approvals gate, kill switch, and signed audit log** that already gates irreversible agent actions (RFC-001 `requireConfirmation`) across the codebase, independent of whatever wallet-provider policy exists underneath.

The question this document answers is what to build or glue on top: a wallet-provisioning layer for the USDC leg, a decision on the ARS leg given what a monotributista can legally do, and a staged rollout that does not get ahead of the regulation.

## 2. Agent-wallet landscape

Coverage below is limited to what the corpus supports. Where the research found nothing on a specific dimension (pricing, KYC), it says so rather than inferring.

### Coinbase CDP: Agentic Wallets

- **Custody model.** Smart-contract wallets (CDP Smart Wallet API) on Base, other EVM chains, and Solana; private keys are generated and held inside a Trusted Execution Environment (TEE) on Coinbase's infrastructure and are never exposed to the agent's prompt or the LLM [verified 3-0: [Coinbase launch page](https://www.coinbase.com/developer-platform/discover/launches/agentic-wallets), corroborated by [CDP non-custodial wallets docs](https://docs.cdp.coinbase.com/wallets/non-custodial-wallets/overview) and [security overview](https://docs.cdp.coinbase.com/wallets/security-and-policies/security-overview)]. Coinbase calls this "non-custodial," but the more accurate label, and the one this document uses, is **vendor-hosted TEE custody**: keys live only on Coinbase-operated infrastructure (no self-hosting), are exportable on request, and trust shifts to Coinbase plus AWS plus the chip vendor rather than being eliminated. A third-party comparison places Coinbase Agentic Wallets in the same "MPC-adjacent custodial" bucket as Circle Wallets on exactly this point [single source: [dev.to custody comparison](https://dev.to/ai-agent-economy/coinbase-agentic-wallets-vs-agent-wallet-sdk-what-the-custody-difference-actually-means-9l)].
- **Spend-policy capabilities.** Configurable per-session and per-transaction caps, address/contract allowlists, and built-in KYT (Know Your Transaction) screening plus automatic OFAC sanctions screening before any transfer is submitted onchain, all enforced server-side before signing [verified 3-0: [Agentic Wallet CLI docs](https://docs.cdp.coinbase.com/agentic-wallet/cli/welcome), [policy engine launch](https://www.coinbase.com/developer-platform/discover/launches/policy-engine)]. Smart-contract-level policy restrictions (constraining arbitrary contract calls, not just transfers) are explicitly marked "coming soon" on Coinbase's own policy-engine page, and no interactive human-approval or escalation primitive is documented: limits are pre-set and auto-enforced, so a project using this stack still has to build its own approval queue for out-of-policy spend [verified 3-0, same sources].
- **x402 and gas.** x402 is native to Agentic Wallets, and Coinbase claims the protocol has processed over 50 million transactions; gasless trades on Base are supported via a paymaster, so the treasury never has to hold ETH for fees [verified 3-0, conservative: independently corroborated and exceeded by [Chainalysis](https://www.chainalysis.com/blog/x402-agentic-payments-adoption/) reporting 100M+ x402 transactions on Base by June 2026].
- **Onboarding.** Developer-self-serve via a CLI (`npx awal`) with a claimed under-2-minutes setup and a pre-built skills library (Authenticate, Fund, Send, Trade, Earn) [single source, primary: launch page]. No pricing, fees, or KYC/KYB requirement is stated on the page; this document treats those as **not covered by this research**.

### Circle: Agent Wallets

- **Custody model.** 2-of-2 MPC on Circle's user-controlled wallets: key shares are never exposed to the agent, and "the user retains custody, and Circle cannot unilaterally move funds without their involvement" [verified 3-0 across multiple independent checks against [Circle's live docs](https://developers.circle.com/agent-stack/agent-wallets)]. This is cooperative custody, not sole custody: Circle holds one of the two key shares and could refuse to co-sign (a freeze capability), a nuance the corpus's adversarial checks call out explicitly but that does not contradict the "cannot unilaterally move funds" wording.
- **Spend-policy capabilities.** Transfer limits, recipient allowlists, and contract blocklists per agent wallet, with limits that can be time-bound (daily or monthly), covering both standard transfers and x402 payments; every transfer is screened against sanctions controls before onchain submission [verified 3-0, multiple independent checks against the same primary doc, corroborated by Circle's Agent Stack launch materials]. As with Coinbase, no documented approval-quorum or human-escalation primitive appears in the corpus for this product.
- **Assets and chains.** USDC is the primary asset for transfers, bridging, and x402 payments, with EURC, other ERC-20 tokens, and native tokens (ETH, MATIC) also supported; Base and Base Sepolia are among the supported chains [verified 3-0].
- **Gas.** Gas-sponsored, but the sponsorship is capped and subject to change [verified 3-0 corroboration].
- **Pricing/KYC.** Not covered by this research.

### Privy

- **Custody model.** Server-side (headless) wallets using TEE plus Shamir's Secret Sharing, with ERC-4337 smart wallets available on EVM chains; acquired by Stripe in June 2025 [single source, primary docs plus one comparison article citing the acquisition date]. Pricing: 499 monthly active users (MAUs) free, then a Core tier at $299/month for up to 2,500 MAUs [single source].
- **Spend-policy capabilities.** A policy engine supporting transfer limits, recipient allowlists/denylists, smart-contract allowlists/denylists, and calldata-level constraints (specific functions and parameters, given the contract ABI), evaluated server-side without custom middleware [verified 3-0].
- **Human-approval support.** m-of-n key quorums natively implement an owner-plus-agent co-signing gate: a wallet can be owned by a quorum where a user key and a server-held authorization key both must sign, which is close to the pattern `ar-agents` wants for approval-gated spend [verified 3-0]. The quorum threshold is a fixed key count, not a value-conditional trigger (there is no native "quorum only above $X" primitive; that requires composing the quorum with Privy's separate value-based policy rules).
- **Corrections (refuted claim).** An earlier draft of this research overstated Privy's guarantee, claiming the TEE evaluates rules "before any signing, transaction, or key-export operation proceeds" and that "the agent cannot bypass policies even if application code is compromised." **This is refuted.** Privy's own docs qualify the first half with "by default," and the same page states transfer-size limits are enforced via transaction simulation that "runs outside the enclave today" (API-level, not TEE-level). The second half is false in Privy's default configuration: policies have an optional `owner_id`; without an owner key set, "the policies can be updated by your app secret alone," meaning a compromised application (which typically holds the app secret) can rewrite or delete its own spend policy via the REST API before transacting, and the enclave will faithfully enforce the attacker's new policy [refuted, high confidence: [Privy policy and controls](https://docs.privy.io/security/wallet-infrastructure/policy-and-controls) contradicted by [Privy authorization keys/owners docs](https://docs.privy.io/controls/authorization-keys/owners/overview) and [policy creation docs](https://docs.privy.io/controls/policies/create-a-policy)]. The corrected, narrow claim that survives: Privy evaluates most, but not all, wallet-action policies inside a TEE before signing, by default, and tamper-resistance against a compromised application is conditional on explicitly configuring an owner key or quorum outside that application's own credentials. Any `ar-agents` integration with Privy must set an external owner/quorum key; skipping that step silently removes the compromise-resistance guarantee.

### Turnkey

- **Custody model.** Non-custodial signing infrastructure (not a full wallet product): keys are generated and signed inside AWS Nitro Enclaves and are never exposed to Turnkey, the integrating application, or its team, with 100 to 150ms signing latency [single source, primary docs].
- **Spend-policy capabilities.** Every action (signing, wallet creation, permission updates) is evaluated by a policy engine inside the enclave before execution, resolving to ALLOW, DENY, or REQUIRES_CONSENSUS, with root-quorum approval thresholds as a native primitive [single source, primary docs]. Turnkey's tenancy model is hierarchical: an Organization for the application, with fully isolated Sub-organizations typically representing an end user, each holding its own enclave-resident HD wallet, which maps directly to one sub-organization per society [single source, primary docs]. Note: `packages/treasury`'s Mural adapter wraps Mural Pay, which is itself built on Turnkey for wallet custody, so Turnkey already sits underneath one rail `ar-agents` uses indirectly [single source: [Mural payout fees docs](https://developers.muralpay.com/docs/payout-developer-fees)].
- **Pricing.** 25 free signatures/month, down to $0.0015/signature at enterprise scale [single source].

### Crossmint

Dual-key smart-contract wallets (owner key plus a TEE-sealed agent key), MiCA CASP-licensed across all 27 EU member states, with KYC/KYB and AML screening, and, notably, the only compared platform giving agents native access to both stablecoin rails and card-network rails (Visa/Mastercard via its `lobster.cash` product); free tier covers 1,000 monthly active wallets [single source, self-reported comparison: [Crossmint's own comparison page](https://www.crossmint.com/learn/agent-wallets-compared), vendor-authored and not independently checked in this corpus]. No Argentina-specific licensing or CVU/ARS integration was found for Crossmint; **not covered by this research**.

### Payman, Skyfire, Stripe Agent Toolkit

These three appear only in blog-level roundups, not primary documentation, in this corpus, and none were run through adversarial verification:

- **Payman**: a spend-management layer with granular budgets and human-in-the-loop approval workflows for large transactions, integrating with LangChain [single source, blog].
- **Skyfire**: agent identity via "Agent Passports" with reputation scores enabling credit-based transactions; the same source notes its ecosystem is newer with fewer integrations than Stripe's [single source, blog].
- **Stripe Agent Toolkit**: single-use virtual cards, metered billing, and payment execution via function calls, with LangChain and CrewAI integrations; Stripe is reported to have added x402 support on Base in February 2026 [single source, blog].

The standard risk pattern articulated across these sources is consistent: never connect an agent directly to a main account; use capped sub-wallets [single source, blog: [Fastio payment APIs roundup](https://fast.io/resources/best-payment-apis-ai-agents/)]. This matches the two-layer design in Section 4.

### x402 ecosystem and market structure

- x402 revives the HTTP 402 status code for machine-to-machine stablecoin payments, was developed by Coinbase, and supports Base, Polygon, Arbitrum, World, and Solana, with EIP-3009 tokens (USDC, EURC) and all ERC-20 tokens via Permit2 [single source, primary: [x402 docs](https://docs.cdp.coinbase.com/x402/welcome)].
- Coinbase's hosted facilitator offers a free tier of 1,000 transactions/month, then $0.001/transaction [single source, primary docs].
- Across broader market-map sources (not individually adversarially checked, but internally consistent with the verified x402/Base claims above): Base is reported as the dominant settlement chain for agent payments, with USDC at roughly 98.6% of agent-payment volume, and x402 dominant at the protocol layer with about 75 million transactions per 30-day window as of the source's last update [single source, blog: [agentpaymentsstack.com](https://agentpaymentsstack.com/)]. By April 2026, Coinbase and third parties reported roughly 69,000 active agents and 165 million-plus cumulative x402 transactions, though cumulative dollar volume is small (about $50 million across those transactions, averaging under $1 per payment), meaning the rail is proven for count and settlement mechanics, not yet for high-value flows [verified, corroborated by CoinDesk's independent reporting on thin micropayment demand: [CoinDesk](https://www.coindesk.com/markets/2026/03/11/coinbase-backed-ai-payments-protocol-wants-to-fix-micropayment-but-demand-is-just-not-there-yet)].
- **Vendor consolidation risk.** 2025 to 2026 saw heavy M&A in this space: Privy acquired by Stripe (June 2025), Dynamic acquired by Fireblocks (October 2025), BVNK acquired by Mastercard (March 2026), and roughly $8.05 billion across seven acquisitions including Capital One to Brex ($5.15B) and Stripe to Bridge ($1.1B) [single source, blog market map: [agentwallet.md](https://agentwallet.md/), [agentpaymentsstack.com](https://agentpaymentsstack.com/)]. This is a reason to avoid deep, hard-to-migrate lock-in to any single wallet vendor at this stage.

## 3. Argentina rails

### Money in: Mercado Pago

MP's Argentina developer reference documents Checkout Pro, Checkout API (Orders), a Payments API, Subscriptions, and QR Code collection, all reachable with a bearer access token, and MP explicitly supports monotributistas (not only personas jurídicas) as collectors [single source, primary: [MP developer reference](https://www.mercadopago.com.ar/developers/es/reference), [MP QR Code docs](https://www.mercadopago.com.ar/developers/es/docs/qr-code/overview)]. That means Naza's own monotributista account can accept ARS today.

Two constraints on using it as the whole ARS rail:

- **No documented money-out API.** The public reference index contains no transfers, payouts, or CVU-to-CVU endpoints; the documented surface is entirely collection- and reporting-centric [single source, primary docs review]. Programmatic disbursement out of MP to arbitrary recipients is not a self-serve capability found in this corpus.
- **Account terms restrict delegation.** MP's own terms (fetched from the Mexico mirror, likely materially similar to the Argentina text but not confirmed identical) state the account is "personal, única e intransferible," that third-party use is prohibited, and that suspected prohibited use can trigger blocking or cancellation [single source, primary docs, unconfirmed jurisdiction match]. This is a contractual constraint independent of BCRA regulation: even where the law permits proxying, MP's own contract may not.

### Money out and CVU/CBU issuance: BIND, Pomelo, and the PSP gate

- **BIND APIBANCO** exposes CVU issuance, alias management, CVU/CBU balance and movement queries, and CVU-to-CVU/CBU transfers, explicitly positioned for automated money movement without human intervention, plus DEBIN and Echeq [single source, primary docs, but retrieved from a November 2025 Wayback Machine snapshot; the live URL did not resolve as of the fetch date, so treat integration specifics as possibly stale]. Access is not self-serve: it requires applying via a contact form to become a BIND client, and the sandbox is gated behind that client status [single source].
- **A separate BIND explainer states the hard compliance constraint directly**: to manage CVUs for third parties, the client must be registered as a PSP with the BCRA [single source, blog: [BIND CVU explainer](https://blog.bind.com.ar/que-es-cvu-fintech/)]. This is the constraint that rules out "one CVU per society, issued by ar-agents" without ar-agents itself becoming a licensed PSP, or partnering with one under the PSPCP-as-a-Service regime described below.
- **Pomelo** offers digital-account and card-issuing APIs with built-in KYC (individuals) and KYB (companies) plus biometric identity validation, meaning an AI agent cannot complete onboarding on its own; it is an enterprise B2B product without public pricing or self-serve onboarding [single source, primary docs].

### Stablecoin on/off ramps

- **Manteca** operates through three named, separately regulated Argentine entities: Sixalime SAU (crypto, PSAV No. 21, CNV-registered), Plus Billetera SA (payments, PSP No. 34,533, BCRA-registered), and Bytelime SAS (FX, Agency No. 20,194, BCRA-registered), all listed as UIF reporting entities [single source, primary docs: [manteca.dev](https://manteca.dev/)]. It offers a Virtual Accounts API (Banking-as-a-Service) covering Argentina, Brazil, Mexico, Colombia, and Peru, which is the most direct candidate this corpus found for provisioning a per-society named account rather than proxying through a personal account. No public fee schedule was found.
- **Bitso** exposes `POST /v3/withdrawals` for ARS payouts to any CBU or CVU, not only the account owner's own; settlement can take up to 24 business hours, moves through PENDING to PROCESSING to COMPLETE/FAILED states, and supports a client idempotency key (`origin_id`) [single source, primary docs: [Bitso withdrawing ARS](https://docs.bitso.com/bitso-payouts-funding/docs/withdrawing-ars)]. This matches `packages/treasury`'s existing `BitsoOffRampAdapter` (including its `deriveOriginId` idempotency helper) closely. The regulated counterparty executing these payouts is Nvio Argentina, Bitso's local PSP [single source].
- **Mural Pay** lets a developer stack a custom per-payout fee (0.5% in the documented example) on top of Mural's own platform exchange fee (0.4% in the same example, for 0.9% total variable fee), settled and paid to the developer monthly, but the fee-stacking feature must be enabled by Mural support first, is not self-serve, and carries corridor-dependent FX spread with no public base pricing [single source, primary docs: [Mural payout fees](https://developers.muralpay.com/docs/payout-developer-fees)].
- A cross-provider treasury-operator guide describes the dominant Argentina pattern as stablecoin on Base into ARS via a licensed off-ramp (Lemon, Ripio, Belo, or similar), with a compliance envelope of KYC/KYB, screening, per-transaction evidence, and maker-checker controls with limits and an audit log, which is architecturally the same shape as the approvals-gate design `ar-agents` already has [single source, blog: [Eco stablecoin B2B guide](https://eco.com/support/en/articles/15575497-stablecoin-b2b-payments-in-latin-america-a-treasury-operator-s-guide)].

### BCRA and UIF constraints (UNVERIFIED in this research pass)

The claims in this subsection come from primary legal sources, the Boletín Oficial and a law-firm analysis, but none of them were run through this corpus's adversarial verification step. Read them as a starting point for legal review, not as settled fact.

- **BCRA Comunicación "A" 8432/2026**, in force since 6 May 2026, creates a new regulated category, "PSPCP como Servicio": PSPs that offer payment accounts (and optionally an interoperable digital wallet) to the clients of a third party, the "tomador del servicio," who access the accounts through the tomador's own interface, technologically integrated to the PSP [UNVERIFIED, not adversarially checked: [Boletín Oficial, 06-05-2026](https://www.boletinoficial.gob.ar/detalleAviso/primera/341644/20260506)]. This is, on its face, exactly the wallet-as-a-service model a per-society CVU would need.
- Under that same Comunicación, a PSPCP cannot serve a tomador until the BCRA has approved that tomador's own registration, must identify the natural persons holding at least 10% of the tomador's capital or voting rights (or otherwise exercising final control), and the tomador must itself be duly incorporated in Argentina, or, if foreign, compliant with the Ley General de Sociedades for habitual local activity [UNVERIFIED]. Read literally, this means a pre-law "sociedad automatizada" that is not yet a duly constituted Argentine legal entity cannot itself be the tomador of a PSPCP-as-a-Service arrangement, and end users of that arrangement are legally considered clients of the PSPCP itself, inheriting the PSPCP's full KYC/AML/onboarding stack [UNVERIFIED, primary: [Allende & Brea analysis](https://allende.com/fintech/el-banco-central-introduce-nuevas-regulaciones-sobre-proveedores-de-servicios-de-pago-05-14-2026/)].
- Separately, the BCRA's PSPCP rulebook states that only personas jurídicas regularly constituted in Argentina may operate as a PSP at all (a persona humana, including a monotributista, cannot register as one), that 100% of client funds must sit at all times in segregated peso sight accounts at Argentine financial entities, immediately available on demand, and that PSPCPs are barred from performing or facilitating client operations with digital assets, including cryptoassets, unless authorized by a competent regulator or the BCRA [UNVERIFIED, primary: [BCRA texto ordenado PSPCP](https://www.bcra.gob.ar/archivos/Pdfs/Texord/t-snp-psp.pdf)]. That last point is the reason the crypto leg of any design must sit with a separately regulated PSAV (Manteca's crypto subsidiary, Bitso via Nvio, Ripio), not inside a payment-account provider itself.
- **UIF Resolución 78/2025**, effective June 2025, requires financial entities to build a forward-looking transactional profile per customer and raises the enhanced-scrutiny threshold for cash deposits and high-value transactions from 20 to 40 Salarios Mínimos Vitales y Móviles (SMVM), above which the entity must identify both the operator and the beneficial owner [UNVERIFIED, primary: [Boletín Oficial, 05-06-2025](https://www.boletinoficial.gob.ar/detalleAviso/primera/326520/20250605)]. The same source notes no blanket simplified-KYC regime exists for fintechs, PSPs, or virtual-asset providers in this text.
- The UIF's separate rules for Proveedores de Servicios de Activos Virtuales (PSAV) cover the crypto leg specifically: registration, KYC, transactional monitoring, suspicious-operation reporting, and Travel Rule obligations including for self-custodied wallets [single source, UNVERIFIED: [Argentina.gob.ar UIF PSAV](https://www.argentina.gob.ar/noticias/normativa-uif-para-proveedores-de-servicios-de-activos-virtuales)].

**Net reading (subject to the caveats above):** an AI-operated, pre-incorporation "sociedad automatizada" cannot itself become a PSP, cannot itself be the tomador of a PSPCP-as-a-Service CVU, and cannot hold pooled ARS funds outside a licensed, segregated-account regime. The only rails a monotributista founder can plausibly use this quarter are ones where a human or an already-incorporated entity is the visible account holder, and where the crypto leg is handled by an already-licensed PSAV rather than by `ar-agents` itself.

## 4. Recommended v1 architecture

### Design

- **Treasury asset: USDC on Base, per society.** This is the one leg of the stack with adversarially verified support: Base is the dominant agent-payment settlement chain, USDC dominates agent-payment volume, and `ar-agents` has already proven x402 USDC intake on Base Sepolia. Anchoring the treasury here means the existing rail-1 work is not wasted and the wallet-provider choice below only has to solve custody and policy, not pick a new chain.
- **Wallet provider: Coinbase Agentic Wallets as primary candidate, Circle Agent Wallets as the credible alternate.** Both have adversarially verified (3-0) support for the properties that matter here: TEE/MPC-based key custody where the agent never touches raw keys, native spend-policy enforcement (session and per-transaction caps, allowlists, time-bound limits), sanctions/KYT screening before every onchain submission, and native x402/USDC/Base support. Coinbase is tighter on the x402 and Base-gasless side (its own protocol, its own paymaster); Circle's cooperative 2-of-2 MPC model gives Circle an explicit freeze capability that Coinbase's design does not document. Neither vendor documents an interactive human-approval or quorum primitive in this corpus (Coinbase's smart-contract-level policy restrictions are "coming soon"; Circle's page does not mention approval quorums), so neither can replace the approvals gate `ar-agents` already has. Decide between the two with a short hands-on integration spike this quarter rather than a desk decision; do not build deep, hard-to-migrate dependencies on either, given the sector's heavy 2025-2026 consolidation.
- **Owner top-ups.** Crypto transfer now (Naza sends USDC on Base directly to a society's wallet); an MP-ARS-to-USDC path via the existing Manteca or Bitso rails later, once there is a documented, reliable ARS-in-to-USDC-out route through one of them.
- **Agent spend: two independent layers, not one.** Layer one is the wallet provider's own policy (session caps, per-transaction limits, allowlists, sanctions screening), enforced at signing, before anything reaches the chain. Layer two is `ar-agents`' existing approvals gate and kill switch (RFC-001 `requireConfirmation`), enforced in application logic, before the provider is ever called. The two layers are necessary because provider policy alone has no documented human-escalation path for either primary candidate, and because the fiat leg (off-ramp conversions, ARS payments) happens entirely outside any wallet provider's visibility, so it needs its own gate regardless of which wallet vendor is chosen.
- **Audit trail: one signed log spans both legs.** Every USDC movement out of the wallet and every ARS off-ramp `convert()`/payment (already logged via the existing `OffRampReceipt`/`OffRampStatusReport` shapes in `packages/treasury`) gets appended to the same signed audit log, because no single vendor in this stack sees both the crypto and the fiat side of a transaction.
- **ARS leg: keep the existing off-ramp adapters, keep the human-titled account.** Do not attempt to give a society its own CVU this quarter; that would require either `ar-agents` becoming a PSP (out of reach for a monotributista, per Section 3, and slow even for an incorporated company) or partnering with an already-licensed PSPCP under the Comunicación A 8432 "PSPCP como Servicio" regime, which itself requires the receiving platform to be a duly incorporated, BCRA-habilitado tomador. Route ARS payouts (via Bitso, Manteca, or Ripio) to Naza's own CBU/CVU or, later, an incorporated company's account, exactly as the current adapters already assume.

### Staged rollout

**v0, this quarter, what a monotributista can actually onboard:**

1. Provision one wallet per society on Base using Coinbase Agentic Wallets (or Circle Agent Wallets, whichever the integration spike favors), on testnet first, then Base mainnet.
2. Owner top-ups by direct USDC transfer from Naza's own wallet or exchange account; no BaaS/CVU-per-society yet.
3. ARS-in continues through Naza's personal monotributista Mercado Pago account (already proven to work for a monotributista collector).
4. ARS-out continues through the existing Bitso/Manteca/Ripio/Mural adapters, settling to Naza's own CBU/CVU, not a society-titled account.
5. Spend policy: wallet-provider session/transaction caps plus the existing `ar-agents` approvals gate and kill switch; every movement into the signed audit log.

**v1, society-owned wallets at scale, gated on either incorporation or a PSPCP-as-a-Service partnership:**

1. Once `ar-agents` (or the relevant operating entity) is incorporated, or a licensed PSPCP contracts to be the "PSPCP como Servicio" for a properly BCRA-habilitado tomador, provision a segregated CVU per society via Manteca's Virtual Accounts API or an equivalent partner.
2. Move from one shared wallet-provider account to per-society sub-accounts (Turnkey-style sub-organizations, or the provider's native per-agent-wallet primitive) once volume justifies the operational overhead.
3. Revisit whether a wallet-layer human-approval quorum (Privy's m-of-n quorums, correctly configured with an external owner key per the correction in Section 2, or Turnkey's `REQUIRES_CONSENSUS`) should sit alongside or replace part of the application-layer approvals gate.

### Open questions for a lawyer

1. Can a monotributista founder legally proxy an AI-operated society's money movement through his own personal MP and bank accounts without breaching MP's "personal, única e intransferible" account terms or triggering "operating on behalf of a third party" exposure, given the society is not itself a persona jurídica yet?
2. Does Comunicación A 8432's incorporation requirement for a PSPCP-as-a-Service "tomador" block any CVU-per-society design entirely until the draft `art. 14` sociedad automatizada regime is live, or is there a reachable pre-law structure (for example, an incorporated holding company acting as tomador on behalf of societies it operates)?
3. When UIF Resolución 78/2025's forward-looking transactional profiling and 40-SMVM enhanced-scrutiny threshold apply, does the account holder (a human) need to disclose to the PSP or the UIF that an autonomous agent, not the human, is initiating the underlying activity?
4. Manteca operates its payments business (Plus Billetera SA, PSP) and its crypto business (Sixalime SAU, PSAV) as separate regulated entities; is that legal separation sufficient insulation for a design that uses both a Manteca-provisioned ARS account and a Manteca-provisioned crypto ramp for the same society, given the PSPCP prohibition on facilitating client crypto operations?
5. What is Naza's personal liability exposure if an autonomous agent, operating through his personal, human-titled account, initiates a payment that later triggers an AML flag or touches a sanctioned counterparty?

### M2-4a: Wallet-provider integration spike (Coinbase Agentic Wallets vs Circle Agent Wallets)
- status: ready
- priority: P1

### M2-4b: Wallet-provider spend policy wired to the existing approvals gate
- status: blocked (depends on M2-4a's provider decision)
- priority: P1
- acceptance: agent-initiated USDC spend above a configurable threshold requires both the chosen wallet provider's own policy check (session/transaction caps, allowlist) to pass AND the existing `requireConfirmation` (RFC-001) approvals gate to clear, with a test proving a spend that passes provider policy but lacks approval is still blocked, and vice versa.

### M2-4c: Unified signed audit log for crypto and fiat legs
- status: ready
- priority: P1
- acceptance: every wallet-provider USDC transfer and every `OffRampAdapter.convert()`/payment (Bitso, Manteca, Ripio, Mural) is appended to the same signed audit log with a common schema (amount, asset, counterparty, txId/externalId, timestamp, approver), reusing `packages/treasury`'s existing `OffRampReceipt`/`OffRampStatusReport` shapes rather than inventing a new one.

### M2-4d: v0 owner top-up flow (manual USDC transfer)

- priority: P2
